# Nutrition & Fueling

## Summary

Nutrition is a trainable skill and one of the most impactful variables in ultrarunning
performance. Most ultrarunners dramatically under-fuel — consuming 80-120 cal/hr versus
the 200-300 cal/hr recommended. The three major coaching voices agree that consistent
caloric intake is essential but diverge sharply on fat adaptation, ketogenic diets, and
the role of fasted training. Getting fueling right can prevent DNFs, while getting it
wrong guarantees them — GI distress is the leading cause of race abandonment.

## Key Concepts

- **Caloric targets**: 200-300 calories per hour during racing, with 30-60g
  carbohydrates per hour as the primary fuel source.

- **Gut training**: The GI system must be trained to absorb fuel under exercise stress,
  just like muscles must be trained to produce force. Practice race nutrition 4-6 times
  during training.

- **Metabolic flexibility**: The ability to efficiently burn both fat and carbohydrate
  at various intensities. All three experts value this, but they disagree on how to
  develop it.

- **Fat adaptation**: Training the body to oxidize fat at higher rates, thereby sparing
  glycogen. Approaches range from occasional fasted runs to full ketogenic diets.

- **Glycogen depletion as signaling**: Low glycogen triggers AMPK pathway activation,
  leading to mitochondrial biogenesis. This creates a tension between fueling for
  performance and fueling for adaptation.

- **Carbohydrate absorption limits**: ~1 g/min from a single carbohydrate source,
  ~1.4 g/min from glucose + fructose combined (dual-transport model).

## What the Experts Say

### Jason Koop (CTS / Training Essentials for Ultrarunning)

- **200-300 cal/hr target**: Koop's primary recommendation, with approximately 90%
  from carbohydrates. "Consume slightly more early (first 2 hrs) as GI symptoms tend
  to develop later" (Ch. 13, citing Costa et al. 2019).

- **30-60g carbs/hr**: Start at 60 g/hr and adjust based on intensity and tolerance.
  Higher intensity requires more carbohydrate. Back-of-pack runners at lower
  intensities need less. "Rates of carbohydrate ingestion up to 90 g/hr are likely
  unnecessary" at typical ultra intensities (Ch. 13).

- **Bullseye nutrition plan**: Develop 3-5 "bullseye" foods through training that
  always work for you:
  - Requirements: at least 1 real food, at least 1 engineered food, something sweet,
    something savory, something salty
  - Second ring: backup foods tried in training
  - Third ring: similar untried foods
  - Off-target: foods known to fail
  - Easy to explain to crew; visual reference
  - Source: Ch. 14, https://trainright.com/bullseye-nutrition-plan-ultramarathon-runners/

- **Separate calories from hydration**: Calories in pocket, hydration in bottles. As
  temperature changes, fluid needs change independently from calorie needs. High-carb
  drinks cause GI distress when fluid intake needs to increase in heat. "You can come
  back from a caloric deficit easily and within minutes by eating. Returning to normal
  hydration status takes much longer" (Ch. 13).

- **Fluid targets**: 16-32 oz fluid per hour depending on conditions. Neither "drink
  as much as tolerable" nor "drink to thirst" is reliable in ultras. Use sweat tests
  to determine individual needs across temperature ranges.

- **Sodium**: 600-800 mg sodium per liter of fluid consumed. Don't need to replace
  100% of sweat sodium — reserves exist in skin; aim for 50-80%. Salt tablets
  generally unnecessary and can encourage overdrinking (Ch. 13).

- **Anti-keto position**: "I do not see compelling evidence for recommending a
  full-time LCHF nutrition strategy for ultramarathon runners" (Ch. 14). Drawbacks:
  - ~8% more oxygen needed to burn fat
  - Limited high-intensity capacity
  - Attenuated training adaptations
  - Increased risk of bone-stress injuries (Heikura et al. 2020)
  - For women specifically: already efficient fat burners, so LCHF is "generally
    ineffective" (Ch. 2)

- **Periodized carbohydrate approach**: Best of both worlds — high carb availability
  for hard sessions, low carb availability for easy sessions (Ch. 14).

- **Gut training**: Practice race-day nutrition in training at least 4-6 times. "The
  equivalent of training your legs at 10 min/mi and then asking them to operate at
  6 min/mi during the race" if you don't practice race nutrition (Ch. 13). GI distress
  is the leading cause of DNFs.

- **Massive under-fueling problem**: Ultrarunners report consuming only 80-120 cal/hr,
  compared to the ~240-260 cal/hr they should be taking in
  (https://trainright.com/ultrarunning-international-society-sports-nutrition-position-stand/).

- **WUT hydration check**: Upon waking, check Weight (W), Urine color (U), Thirst (T).
  If 2 of 3 indicate dehydration, you're likely dehydrated. Use over a rolling 3-day
  period for trends (Ch. 13).

- **Protein during long efforts**: 40g protein every 4 hours throughout the day during
  heavy training. Carries particular weight for runs over 4 hours (Ch. 13).

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)

- **Metabolic flexibility**: Johnston emphasizes the ability to use both fuel sources
  efficiently rather than dietary restriction. A lean, well-trained athlete has
  ~100,000 calories stored as fat vs. ~2,000 calories as glycogen. "Metabolic
  flexibility over restriction" is the core philosophy
  (https://uphillathlete.com/nutrition/fat-adaptation/).

- **Fat adaptation benefits**: New studies show fat usage is 2x higher in well-trained
  athletes, sustained to maximum intensities. Two advantages:
  - Virtually limitless fuel supply
  - Reduced lactate production at submaximal efforts = lower perceived exertion
  - Source: Ch. 2

- **Fat oxidation rates by training level**:
  - Untrained: 0.56 g/min at 49% VO2max
  - Well-trained endurance: 0.67-0.84 g/min at 55-63% VO2max
  - Well-trained on LCHF diet: 1.15-1.74 g/min at 65% VO2max
  - Source: https://uphillathlete.com/nutrition/fat-adaptation/

- **Fasted training — cautious approach**: Johnston recommends doing "some aerobic
  capacity-building workouts in a fasted state (morning before calories)" to force
  muscles to better utilize available fat. Athletes training >12 hours/week will see
  less effect from dietary modification since training volume provides ample stimulus.
  "Becoming fat-adapted is one of the simplest to implement training prescriptions
  you will read in this book" (Ch. 2).

- **Fasted training warnings for women**: Johnston explicitly warns against fasted
  training for female athletes. Health risks of LCHF for athletes include menstrual
  dysfunction, compromised bone health, and impaired immunity — especially in females
  (https://uphillathlete.com/nutrition/high-fat-low-carb-diet-ultra-endurance-performance/).

- **LCHF evidence is mixed**: Elite ultra-marathoners on LCHF showed 2.3-fold higher
  peak fat oxidation (up to 1.9 g/min). However, a study on elite race walkers showed
  the LCHF group had 3.3% (86-second) slower race time. LCHF reduces exercise economy
  despite increasing fat oxidation capacity. "There is no conclusive evidence
  supporting consistent performance enhancement, even at moderate exercise intensities"
  (https://uphillathlete.com/nutrition/high-fat-low-carb-diet-ultra-endurance-performance/).

- **Practical fueling**:
  - Pre-workout: Eat 2 hours before (carbs + protein, e.g., toast with PB and banana)
    or 30 min before (quick carbs only)
  - During workout: Body has glycogen for ~90 minutes; beyond that, must consume carbs
    to avoid bonking and muscle breakdown
  - Post-workout: Carbs + protein together
  - Daily: Don't go more than 3-5 hours awake without eating carbs + protein
  - Source: https://uphillathlete.com/nutrition/nutrition-101-part-two-putting-it-into-practice/

- **Post-exercise recovery nutrition**: Consume 100-200 calories in the first 30 min
  post-exercise (if over 1 hour). Ratio: 3:1 or 4:1 carbohydrates to protein. Missing
  this window can delay recovery by days. Eat extra protein before bed for recovery
  (Ch. 3).

- **UTMB fueling example**:
  - Over 15,000 calories across 40+ hours
  - Between major crew points, carry 2,000+ calories (sections exceed 10 hours)
  - Nearly 1,000 calories consumed at single aid station stops
  - Electrolyte deficiency caused dizziness/nausea; fixed with salt caps and salty soup
  - Palette fatigue is inevitable — novelty foods (pizza!) can restore appetite
  - Source: https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/fueling-for-utmb/

- **Diet philosophy**: Kilian via Johnston: recommends not being crazy about nutrition
  but eating enough and balanced. "Food is fuel. You need to eat with an eye toward
  recovery" (Ch. 3).

- **Weight warning**: Rico Elmer: "I am 5'10", best weight 154 lbs. Went to 143 lbs —
  wasn't nearly as fast and getting sick all the time" (Ch. 3). Underfueling degrades
  both performance and health.

### David Roche & Dr. Megan Roche (SWAP / The Happy Runner)

- **"Eat enough, always. Eat too much, sometimes. Eat too little, never."** The Roches'
  core nutrition mantra for runners. There is no one-size-fits-all diet, but this
  overarching principle applies to every happy runner. The emphasis is on abundance, not
  restriction. (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **Energy availability as the #1 determinant of longevity.** Negative energy
  availability (running a caloric deficit) is one of the biggest roadblocks to
  performance — increased injury rate, fewer adaptations to training stimuli, and
  shortened careers. "Energy availability is the number one determinant of longevity in
  running." (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **The power-to-weight fallacy.** Dr. Megan Roche's personal story: as a collegiate
  track athlete at Duke, she was seduced by the power-to-weight ratio equation. It led
  to disordered eating and a persistent catabolic state. Her epiphany came at the
  Stanford Invitational, watching professional runners who were "strong and durable" —
  it took healthy energy availability, not low weight, to have a successful running
  career. She "ordered pepperoni pizza with ranch dressing and never looked back."
  (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **The dinner table epiphany.** At the 2015 World Long Distance Mountain Running
  Championships in Zermatt, the Roches observed the best ultrarunners eating
  enthusiastically — olive oil by the pour, extra-large plates of pasta. One top runner
  said: "The dinner table is a great way to tell how long a runner's career will be. The
  eaters last. The skimmers may be fast, but probably not for long." (*The Happy Runner*,
  Ch. 8)

- **Caloric deficit as a ticking time bomb.** Initial weight loss may boost performance
  temporarily, but reverting to negative energy availability causes performance to
  diminish even more quickly. "The fuse seems to get shorter each time." It creates a
  mental-physical cycle of calorie restriction that can be very difficult to overcome and
  may never completely leave. (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **All food is good food.** Labeling foods as off-limits can lead to disordered eating
  behaviors — or be a stand-in for eating disorders. "Food is fuel, sure. But it's also
  friends and family and fun." Rather than restricting, set up a framework and support
  system that helps embrace all the good stuff about food. (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **Four keys to healthy eating behaviors** (SWAP framework):
  1. Eliminate tools of self-judgment — unless losing weight at a doctor's direction,
     disregard the scale. "Pick it up and toss it in the trash."
  2. Understand triggering events — comments like "you look stronger" can trigger body
     image concerns.
  3. Reinforce that all food is good food — well-balanced, nutrient-dense, and delicious.
  4. Understand you are not alone — talk openly with partners, family, medical
     professionals.
  (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **Iron and nutrition** (Dr. Megan Roche): Iron is essential for hemoglobin and oxygen
  transport. All runners lose iron through foot-strike hemolysis. Women additionally lose
  iron through menstruation. Meat intake matters because "heme" iron from red meat is
  more readily absorbed than plant-based non-heme iron. Female vegetarian runners may
  need to think more carefully about their iron intake than male vegetarians. Men
  generally have more margin for error on iron status. (*The Happy Runner*, Ch. 8)

- **120-140g carbs/hour during ultras**: David Roche consumed 120-140 grams of
  carbohydrates per hour during his Leadville 100 course-record run (15:26:34 in 2024).
  This is significantly higher than the traditional 60-90g/hr recommendation and even
  exceeds Koop's 30-60g/hr guideline. Roche's reasoning: "At altitude and during intense
  efforts, you're burning through so many calories, and the only way to make it possible
  is by taking in a ton of fuel." This aligns with newer sports science research pushing
  carb intake higher for elite ultrarunners operating at high intensities. Note: this
  level of intake requires extensive gut training and may not be appropriate for all
  athletes — Roche is an elite with years of gut-training practice. (Source: iRunFar
  interview)

- **Sweat rate individuality**: David Roche has "literally the highest sweat rate we have
  ever measured," underscoring that hydration strategy must be individualized based on
  personal sweat rate data. His extreme sweat rate, rather than carbohydrate availability
  alone, may partly explain his ultrarunning success — high fluid turnover necessitates
  aggressive hydration and creates a larger window for caloric absorption. Practical
  takeaway: athletes should get sweat rate testing rather than relying on generic fluid
  intake guidelines. (Source: iRunFar interview)

- **Nutrition education and bone stress injury prevention** (Dr. Megan Roche): A 7-year
  Stanford study led by Dr. Michael Fredericson with Megan Roche examined nutrition
  education's effectiveness in preventing bone stress injuries (BSIs) among NCAA Division
  I female endurance runners. Key findings:
  - Nutrition education reduced trabecular bone injuries from 0.18 to 0.10 events per
    person-year
  - At one institution, overall BSI rates dropped from 0.63 to 0.27 events per
    person-year
  - Results varied by institution — "team culture surrounding fueling, health, and
    performance would impact study success and athlete buy-in" (Dr. Megan Roche)
  - Education alone wasn't sufficient; role model stories about experiencing the Triad
    were needed to shift perceptions and drive behavior change
  - Practical implication: "an athlete's strongest self is their healthiest and often
    fastest self" — reinforcing the Roches' core message that adequate energy availability
    is a performance advantage, not a sacrifice
  (Source: Stanford Human Performance Alliance)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)

- **Glycogen depletion as signaling**: Low glycogen stores trigger the AMPK signaling
  pathway, leading to mitochondrial biogenesis and metabolic adaptations. "If the
  marathon is an event that is dependent on the optimal fuel use ratio between
  carbohydrates and fats, how do we signal the body to change that ratio so that
  there is more reliance on fat? We need to 'embarrass' it and send the signal that
  more fat needs to be used" (Ch. 9).

- **Carbs during runs blunt adaptation**: "Taking carbohydrate during a run vs. taking
  nothing changes the subsequent signaling pathway response entirely" (Ch. 9). If
  glycogen depletion is the signal for fuel-system adaptation, consuming carbs during
  a long run may blunt that signal. Practical implication: for *some* long runs
  targeting fuel adaptation, forgo carbohydrate intake.

- **Training in depleted state**: Training in glycogen-depleted state enhances gene
  transcription of AMPK and MAPK signaling proteins — both control mitochondrial
  biogenesis:
  - Hansen (2005): twice-every-other-day group showed significantly better
    time-to-exhaustion + increased enzyme activity vs. once-every-day group
  - Yeo (2008): increased CS, HAD enzymes and fat oxidation
  - Source: Ch. 9-10

- **Long runs as fuel system training**: The long run is the ONLY workout that stresses
  total fuel supply enough to trigger glycogen storage increases and fat utilization
  improvements. The first portion depletes stores, the second portion at faster pace
  forces fuel system adaptations (Ch. 18).

- **Marathon fuel management**: "The main cause of fatigue though is the depletion of
  fuel. While we never fully run out of fuel, it is the anticipation of running out
  of fuel that ultimately causes the slowing" (Ch. 15). The brain runs an anticipatory
  fuel calculation and adjusts pace accordingly.

- **Substrate efficiency**: "If we can get the body to change its fueling ratio at
  marathon-specific speeds, then performance will be improved" (Ch. 18). Long runs
  with pickups/progressions accomplish this by depleting stores first and forcing
  adaptations second.

- **Post-exercise**: 100-200 calories in first 30 min post-exercise. 3:1 or 4:1
  carb:protein ratio. Miss this window and recovery can be delayed by days. Growth
  hormone: ~550% increase from 0-40 min of easy running, then only 40-50% more from
  40-60 min (Ch. 19).

## Where They Agree

1. **Consistent fueling matters enormously.** All four sources recognize that
   under-fueling is one of the most common mistakes in ultrarunning, leading to bonks,
   GI distress, and DNFs. The Roches go furthest, framing adequate energy availability
   as the #1 determinant of career longevity.

2. **Post-exercise nutrition window.** Johnston, Koop, and Magness agree on consuming
   carbs + protein within 30-60 minutes after training, at a roughly 3:1 or 4:1
   carb:protein ratio.

3. **Fat adaptation has value — as one tool.** Johnston, Koop, and Magness acknowledge
   that trained athletes oxidize fat more efficiently and that this matters for
   ultra-distance events. None advocates a pure carbohydrate-only approach.

4. **Full ketogenic diets are not recommended.** Despite different emphasis, Johnston,
   Koop, and Magness all have concerns about LCHF as a permanent dietary strategy for
   competitive athletes.

5. **Metabolic flexibility is the goal.** Johnston, Koop, and Magness all prefer an
   athlete who can efficiently burn both fat and carbohydrate over one locked into a
   single fuel pathway.

6. **Under-eating is more dangerous than over-eating.** Johnston (Rico Elmer's weight
   loss warning), Koop (under-fueling data), and the Roches ("eat too little, never")
   converge on the same message: chronic caloric deficit degrades performance and
   health. The Roches add that the damage can be cumulative and progressively harder
   to reverse.

7. **Food should not be a source of anxiety.** Johnston (via Kilian: "don't be crazy
   about nutrition") and the Roches ("food is friends and family and fun") both push
   back against obsessive or restrictive food cultures in endurance sports.

## Where They Disagree

- **Fasted training**: Johnston recommends fasted morning aerobic workouts as a
  practical tool for improving fat oxidation. Magness provides the scientific rationale
  for training in a depleted state (AMPK/MAPK signaling). Koop does not explicitly
  recommend fasted training and instead advocates a periodized carbohydrate approach
  (high carb for hard sessions, low carb for easy sessions) — a more moderate position.

- **Carbs during long runs**: Magness argues that consuming carbohydrates during
  training runs can blunt fuel-system adaptations and recommends occasionally forgoing
  them. Koop's position is the opposite: gut training requires practicing race-day
  nutrition during training, meaning you should be taking in calories during long runs.
  Johnston occupies a middle ground, recommending fasted workouts for adaptation but
  proper fueling during longer efforts.

- **Ketogenic diets**: Koop calls LCHF "generally ineffective" and highlights risks
  including bone-stress injuries and blunted training adaptations. Johnston presents
  the mixed evidence more neutrally — noting both the 2.3x higher fat oxidation and
  the 3.3% performance decrement — but warns against it for women. Magness focuses on
  the signaling value of glycogen depletion without advocating a permanent dietary
  change.

- **How much science vs. simplicity**: Koop provides the most specific, numbers-driven
  protocol (200-300 cal/hr, 30-60g carbs/hr, 600-800mg sodium/L, Bullseye plan).
  Johnston is more philosophical ("food is fuel, eat balanced"). Magness is most
  focused on the underlying signaling science rather than practical race-day protocols.
  The Roches are the most relationship-oriented — they focus on the athlete's
  psychological relationship with food first and trust that adequate fueling follows
  from a healthy mindset.

- **Weight and body composition**: Koop, Johnston, and Magness discuss body composition
  as a performance variable (with appropriate caveats). The Roches take the strongest
  position against self-monitoring of weight, advising athletes to throw away the scale
  unless directed otherwise by a doctor. They argue that the performance gains from
  weight loss are almost always outweighed by the risks of disordered eating and
  negative energy availability.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **New ultrarunner with no fueling plan**: Start with Koop's Bullseye plan. Establish
   200-300 cal/hr and 30-60g carbs/hr as baseline targets. Practice race nutrition
   during 4-6 training runs before the event. Separate calories from hydration.

2. **Athlete with chronic GI issues in races**: Prioritize gut training (Koop). Start
   with lower caloric density and gradually increase. Track what works and what doesn't.
   Consider Johnston's advice on simplicity and real food. Avoid high-fructose loads if
   menopausal (Koop).

3. **Athlete interested in fat adaptation**: Use Johnston's approach — occasional fasted
   morning aerobic workouts (Z1-Z2 only) to improve fat oxidation. Never do fasted
   high-intensity sessions. If female, be extra cautious and monitor menstrual function.
   Do NOT adopt a full ketogenic diet for race performance.

4. **Athlete seeking training adaptation from long runs**: Apply Magness's signaling
   framework selectively. On some training long runs (not all), reduce or eliminate
   carb intake to maximize AMPK signaling. Always fuel properly on race-simulation
   runs and in races themselves.

5. **Athlete consistently bonking in races**: Almost certainly under-fueling. Use
   Koop's data: most ultrarunners consume 80-120 cal/hr vs. the 200-300 cal/hr they
   need. Increase intake frequency (every 20-30 min rather than waiting for hunger).
   Front-load calories in early miles when GI function is best.

6. **100-mile+ race preparation**: Follow Johnston's UTMB approach — plan for 15,000+
   calories across the event. Know exactly how many calories you'll carry between each
   crew point. Include variety to combat palette fatigue. Practice eating solid food
   while moving. Use Koop's aid station strategy for efficiency.

7. **Female athlete**: Be cautious with fasted training (Johnston's explicit warning).
   Monitor menstrual function as a canary for RED-S (Koop). Already efficient fat
   burners — LCHF diets offer diminishing returns (Koop, Ch. 2). Focus on adequate
   energy availability over dietary restriction. Pay special attention to iron status —
   female runners lose iron through both foot-strike hemolysis and menstruation; female
   vegetarians need particular care (Dr. Megan Roche).

8. **Athlete showing signs of disordered eating or body image concerns**: Apply the
   Roches' four keys — eliminate tools of self-judgment (scale), identify triggering
   events, reinforce that all food is good food, and emphasize they are not alone.
   Recommend talking to a medical professional. Frame energy availability as a
   performance variable: "the eaters last." Never frame weight loss as a training goal.

9. **Athlete asking about weight loss for performance**: Present Dr. Megan Roche's
   personal story and the dinner table epiphany as counterweights. Initial gains from
   caloric deficit are a "ticking time bomb" — the fuse gets shorter each time. Redirect
   to the Roches' mantra: "eat enough, always; eat too much, sometimes; eat too little,
   never." If weight management is medically indicated, defer to a physician.
